    • From Virtual Concept to Physical Artefact: A Portfolio on the Development Process of an Artificial Robotic Hand

    Welcome to my Robotic Modelling and Drone Skin Design portfolio!

    My name is Georgiana Coroiu and I am a third-year AI and Robotics student at Staffordshire University. This portfolio presents the development process of a robotic hand.

    1. Introduction

    Human hands have a great potential for holding and manipulating different objects in a dexterous way, a skill that can be acquired by training. Therefore, a multi-fingered robot hand can provide a great chance to achieve such dexterity in a robotic system, as well as the ability to explore, touch and get the perception of physical properties, such as temperature, weight, etc (Melchiorri and Kaneko, 2008, pp.345‐360).

    2. Background

    Given the importance of the human hand’s functions, there has always been a great interest shown in capturing and replicating the complexity of a hand, and science and engineering have been trying to match the hand’s functions and reproduce artificial robotic hands for decades. An example is Shadow Dexterous Hand which can be found in Figure 1. Even though many researchers have been focusing on this subject, there are a limited number of real-world applications in the robotics industry. Artificial hands show great potential in many different domains, such as prosthetics, assistive robotics, supervised manipulation, teleoperation, etc. (Piazza et al., 2019).

    Based on the research conducted by Piazza et al. (2019), four different types of joints were identified (Figure 2):
    • Rigid: a system connected by fixed mechanical elements.
    • Flexible: a system having the links connected using flexible elements.
    • Dislocatable: a system connected using elastic and flexible elements.
    • Soft continuous: a system containing continuously flexible materials.

    2.1 Aim of the project

    The main objective of this project is to create a robotic hand that can replicate the flexibility, and various degrees of freedom of a human hand, as well as its appearance. This portfolio will discuss and analyse the development process, outlining the key steps and the decisions made through the process, along with the reasoning behind them. The main focus will be on designing and creating the robotic artificial skin, the decisions that led to choosing the materials and the techniques used.

    3. Analysis and Design

    This section will discuss the technologies that have been considered to develop a prototype and, later, the artefact. Moreover, it will discuss the decisions made throughout the designing phase, as well as the prototypes that have been developed.

    3.1. 3D Scanning

    3D scanning is a technology used for designing and inspecting a physical model, by converting it into digital 3D Computer-Aided Design (CAD) using various scanning software. Then, the virtual image can be studied in depth, modified, stored, or printed, which allows preparing for production by ensuring that the initial vision has been realised as intended, as Javaid et al. (2021) describes in their research paper. Once the scanning is done, the CAD file can be exported into different file formats, such as Standard Triangulate Language (STL). 3D scanning uses advanced software for precise measurement and storage to help increase the process flexibility and reliability and it can be used in numerous domains, such as reverse engineering, education, architecture, healthcare, industrial tools development, quality monitoring, prototyping, etc., allowing to use different software such as “Colin 3D, Artec Studio, PolyWorks, Inspect Plus and Volume Graphics” (Javaid et al., 2021).

    For this project, the 3D scanning tool was considered to scan, and later 3D print, the internal structure of the hand. Below, the scan of the hand can be found and it should be mentioned that some imperfections can be observed due to the real hand's movement.

    3.2. 3D Printing

    3D printing is a technology that allows the creation of physical objects by adding material successively and it can be used for improving the manufacturing process's efficiency in various sectors, such as healthcare, automotive and aerospace industries, agriculture, and many others. The three-dimensional structure can be printed from Computer-Aided Design designs (Shahrubudin, Lee and Ramlan, 2019).

    For this project, it was proposed to print the internal structure for the prototype (Figure 6).

    3.3. Skin materials and production

    According to the research conducted by Cirillo, Cirillo and Pirozzi (2012), the decisions regarding the development of artificial skin should be made based on a set of requirements, some of the most important ones being high scalability, fast response, light-weight, low cost, low power consumption and ability to apply the skin to different curved surfaces, therefore high conformability. This section will discuss some of the most common materials and techniques used in the industry to imitate human skin, mentioning some of their advantages and disadvantages.

    Even though cell cultures (reproduction of human skin cells in vitro) simulating skin are available and are being researched further, they are expensive, and hard to store and work with. Therefore, the analysis performed by Dąbrowska et al. (2015) shows that most of the time, “the materials used to imitate human skin are gelatinous substances, elastomers, epoxy resin, liquid suspensions and even metals and textiles“. Usually, skin models are used for testing, teaching, calibration, etc. and using biologically inactive materials is preferred to using biologically active materials, as they are more reliable and durable, cheaper and, also, they are cheaper and easier to store and work with, as previously mentioned, and the synthetic skin does not raise ethical issues, which is an essential point to consider (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    As with most development processes, the skin development process usually consists of four phases as well: defining requirements, choosing materials, manufacturing, and evaluation. When choosing the materials to be used, it is important to consider a few requirements such as cost, durability, environmental impact, and practicality, as well as the purpose of manufacturing a skin model and the properties and functions wanted (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    Liquid suspensions represent a material that is useful when simulating the surface and the appearance of the skin, rather than the mechanical properties. “Suspensions of lipid, polymeric, and inorganic particles can be added in liquids such as water, milk or oils to obtain scattering properties similar to those of skin”, as the authors mention (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    Gelatinous substances are materials that can interact with water, allowing it to control some of the properties, such as elasticity, hardness and surface properties. These substances can be:

    • Gelatine is used to simulate human tissue more than the actual skin and it provides absorption, density and stiffness properties similar to the ones of human skin (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    • Polyvinyl alcohol gels (PVA) represent a synthetic polymer, usually used for “medical applications, such as testing, optimisation or development of imaging-based diagnostic techniques”, for example, ultrasound systems (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    Elastomers are polymers that can be used to simulate the viscoelastic and physical properties of human skin and they can be “silicones, polyurethanes, polyether block amides, polyisoprene, and polybutadiene”, as mentioned in the same excellent overview of the materials used for skin models (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    • Silicones are polymers containing Silicon (Si), Oxygen (O), Carbon (C), and Hydrogen (H) as main elements and they can be mixed with fillers to strengthen and tune their properties. Skin models containing silicones have a long lifespan and they are easy to manipulate, they provide long-term stability and can simulate multiple human skin properties (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    • Polyurethanes can be used to create tactile-sensing robotic skin, as they can be used with polyurethane elastomers to influence the hardness properties (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    Epoxy resins are used to obtain cross-linked or thermoset plastics, but they can also be used for manufacturing skin models, to be utilized in optical tomography or as a top layer of breast phantoms for X-ray imaging (Dąbrowska et al., 2015).

    3.4. Materials comparison and selection

    For this project, it was decided that Polyurethane resin to be used to make the first cast of the hand and the mould for the silicone pouring phase, as it is a low-cost material, therefore it meets the project budget requirements, it is durable and an easy to work with material that does not require prior experience, as long as instructions are followed, and most importantly, it has been made available by the university.

    For manufacturing the skin, it was decided for silicone materials to be used due to similar reasons previously mentioned: its low cost, availability, durability, and easy manipulation.

    3.5. Conclusion

    As previously mentioned, it was decided that, for developing the skin model, resin and silicone materials, along with any required additional materials.

    Regarding the internal structure, it was decided that 3D printing cannot be accomplished within the given timeframe, due to the limited collaboration of group members, therefore it was decided that the internal structure to be manufactured using metal wires and wood, as this is a faster and more cost-effective way to manufacture it and, since the skin model is the main focus, this internal structure will serve the purpose of showing the flexibility and the various degrees of freedom of the final artefact. More about developing the artefact can be found in the “Development” section.

    The final artefact should have an appropriate texture and colour and the fingers should be flexible.

    4. Development

    This section will focus on the development process of the artefact and will discuss the decisions regarding the tools and technologies that were used to develop the artificial hand. Moreover, as mentioned before, the main focus will be on developing the skin of the artefact, as well as the materials and techniques that were used.

    4.1. Moulding the hand

    The first step of creating the skin is casting the hand. This is realised by using Chromatic Moulding Alginate material, a powder that is ideal for casting body parts, and once mixed with water, it takes around 3-4 minutes to set (Figure 7).

    Before casting the hand, it is necessary to grease the body part with an oily agent to protect the hand, making it simpler to be removed and prevent the mould from being disturbed. Once this is done, the moulding material is mixed with water until smooth and the hand can be inserted into the mixture for approximately two minutes. Once the alginate is set, the hand can be carefully removed. This process, together with the result, can be visualised in Figures 8, 9 and 10.

    4.2. Casting the hand

    The next step is to cast the hand with resin. The materials used are “Xencast P2 Fast Cast Polyurethane resin”, a two-part polyurethane casting resin, and green pigment (Figure 11).

    The two resin parts need to be mixed in equal quantities and, then the pigment can be added and mixed in as well. Once the composition is ready, it can be poured into the mould and left to set for approximately an hour. The process and the result of this phase can be found in Figure 12 and Figure 13.

    Once the resin has been cured, the alginate can be removed, and the cast hand can be visualised in Figure 14.

    4.3. Second mould

    The next phase is to develop a two-part mould that will be used to cast the hand's skin. For making the second mould, the same materials as the ones used for casting the hand will be utilized, specifically the resin. Firstly, a square-shaped container was made from wood pieces and the cast was glued in the middle of the container, as it can be observed in Figure 15.

    Once the hand is fixed, the pouring process can start. This is divided into two phases: the first step consists of pouring half of the resin, until it covers the bottom half of the hand, and when cured, more resin can be poured, until it completely covers the hand. This process can be visualised in Figure 16.

    To prevent the hand from sticking to the mould and becoming unable to be removed, a sprayable release agent, designed for releasing polyurethanes and epoxy resins (Figure 17), must be used between the two pouring sessions, after the first layer of resin is cured. For the same reasons, pieces of wax can be placed on the surface of the first layer, as well as lubricating the casted hand with Petroleum Jelly (Figure 18).

    Once the second layer of resin is cured as well, the two halves of the mould can be pulled apart.

    4.4. Manufacturing the internal structure

    For the internal structure, the following materials have been used:

    • Wires for the fingers;

    • One piece of wood for the palm;

    • One bold for support;

    • Glue.

    There are two types of wires used: one thick aluminium wire for the main structure and one thin copper wire to support them (Figure 19). For each finger, two pieces of aluminium wire have been cut, being equal to the finger’s length, and have been twisted using a drill, then a copper wire used to twist it around the main structure to improve their durability (Figure 20).

    For the palm, a piece of wood has been cut and adjusted to match a human’s palm form and the fingers’ structures have been glued into the wood piece, as well as a bold that serves as a support. The final result can be visualised in Figure 21.

    By manufacturing this internal structure, the finger will be able to move and bend, offering the robotic hand the flexibility and various degrees of freedom of a human’s hand.

    4.5. Skinning process

    The final phase represents skin manufacturing. The material used to cast the skin is Platsil Gel 00 silicone, pigment and lighter fluid (Figure 22). Before pouring the silicone, the mould has to be very well cleaned with a special solution for cleaning mould, named “MC 1 Mould Cleaner” (Figure 22).

    Additionally, the internal structure must be covered with a special non-sticky tape: soft foam underwrap (Figure 23), a pre-wrap tape that ensures that the silicone sticks to the internal structure. This phase can be found in Figure 24.

    The silicone pouring phase is divided in two steps as it will be poured in two coats. Once the materials are prepared, the two-part silicone can be mixed with the pigment in a small quantity, and lighter fluid should be added to the mixture, as it helps the material flow better in the skinning process. The two parts of the mould are coated and well covered with silicone, as well as the internal structure, as shown in Figure 25 and Figure 26.

    After approximately an hour, when the silicone is cured, a larger amount of silicone can be mixed with a substance called “deadener”, which is used to simulate the look and feel of skin (Figure 27). To hold the mould together, two clumps are used and the silicone can be poured into the mould containing the internal structure (Figure 28).

    Once the silicone has been cured, the mould can be removed and the result can be visualised in Figure 29.

    To enhance the aesthetic appeal, the silicone excess has been removed and artificial nails have been attached. To do this, fake nails have been purchased and to suit the hand appearance, the nails have been filed and shortened. Finally, the ultimate form of the model hand can be found in Figure 30 and Figure 31.

    5. Evaluation

    The final artefact meets all requirements defined in the design phase, regarding skin, as the colour and texture are appropriate. The fingers can be bent, but because there were wires used for the internal structure, the fingers can bend backwards, which ideally should not happen, as it is not natural for a human hand to bend this way, therefore this aspect could be improved by redesigning the internal structure of the hand in the future.

    The appearance of the final artefact could be improved to make the model hand’s surface smoother, by using a silicone smoothing agent and once cured, a silicone finish can be applied. The model can be painted to represent veins and other details to improve the presentation of the model.
